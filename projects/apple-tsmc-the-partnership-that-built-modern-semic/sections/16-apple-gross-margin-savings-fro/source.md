### Intel Out, $4B+ In

The decision to abandon Intel for the Mac was the single most profitable supply chain decision in Apple’s history.

Prior to 2020, Apple paid Intel approximately $250 for a possibly overheating laptop CPU. Today, the M-series chip costs Apple estimated $40-50 to manufacture.

The Mac gross margin story is the clearest proof of Apple’s silicon thesis. In 2019, Mac GM sat at 28.5%, constrained by Intel’s $150+ processor ASPs and a roadmap that delivered clock speed regression instead of progress. By 2025, Mac GM stands at 39.5%. That 11 percentage point expansion on a $40B Mac business translates to roughly $4.4 billion in incremental annual gross profit.

Intel’s failure to ship 10nm at competitive yields for over five years represented a multi-billion dollar transfer of value from Cupertino to Santa Clara. Apple finally recaptured it. The M-series chips cost Apple an estimated $77 per unit in COGS (N3 wafers, packaging, yield loss) versus Intel’s $150+ Core i9 ASP. At 65 million Mac units annually, the transition generates $4.77 billion in gross profit lift. Break-even is 14 million units against a $1 billion NRE investment.

### The Modem Bet: $6B to Break Qualcomm’s Stranglehold

The ongoing transition away from Qualcomm (estimated $1.2B/year savings) represents the final major piece of the puzzle.

The C1 modem represents Apple’s most expensive and most contentious insourcing bet. Qualcomm’s X75 5G chip commands a $20+ ASP with gross margins of 50%. Apple’s C1 alternative, built on N4 with a 50mm² die, costs an estimated $14.40 in COGS. The $5 per-unit savings across 250 million cellular iOS devices yields $1.2B in annual gross profit improvement.

The real story is the NRE. Apple spent $6B (including the $1B Intel modem acquisition, 2,200 engineers, mask sets, and qualification costs) to get here. Break-even volume is 1.3B units, roughly 5 years of iPhone production. By 2027, when Qualcomm is fully phased out, the recurring savings become pure margin expansion.

### Wi-Fi 7 In-House: $700M Recaptured from Broadcom

Apple’s move against Broadcom in Wi-Fi and Bluetooth receives less attention but carries equal significance. The N1 chip, Apple’s first in-house Wi-Fi 7 solution, replaces Broadcom’s $11 combo chip with an internally designed alternative costing $8.7 in COGS. At $2.3 savings per unit across 300 million iOS devices, that’s $700M in annual gross profit recapture. The NRE was relatively modest at $250M, yielding break-even at 110 million units. Breakeven likely within the first year of production.

### Gross Margin Savings

These savings recur and compound annually. Each A-series generation inherits prior design wins. Each M-series chip amortizes NRE across Mac, iPad, and now Vision Pro. The $7.5B in combined annual savings (Intel CPU: $5B, Qualcomm Modem: $1.2B, Broadcom Connectivity: $700M, GPU/NPU IP: $0.5B, plus efficiency gains) funds the next round of R&D. Apple plows savings back into R&D. Android OEMs still pay Qualcomm and MediaTek for chips Apple now makes itself.

The Total Product Gross Margin trajectory tells the story: 34.4% in 2018 to 36.8% in 2025, with Mac leading the charge and iPhone following as custom silicon matures.

More Apple in-house chips mean more business for TSMC. M-series boosted TSMC’s Apple revenue by $5B. The C-series and N-series wafers will incrementally add up to $3B.
