For most of TSMC’s advanced node history, Apple was the undisputed volume leader. On N5, Apple’s quarterly wafer demand consistently exceeded the combined demand from NVIDIA, Broadcom, Amazon, and AMD through 2023. That hierarchy has now inverted. By 4Q25, NVIDIA alone consumed more N5 wafers than Apple’s entire A-series and M-series combined. The gap widened on N3. Apple ramped N3 first, taking 92% of node capacity in 2023, but AI accelerators followed with overwhelming force. By 2Q26, AI ecosystem N3 demand (led by NVIDIA’s Blackwell successors) exceeds Apple’s N3 demand by nearly 3x. The crossover happened in under 18 months.

The composition of AI demand makes the shift even more striking. NVIDIA alone is projected to consume more wafers on N3 by 4Q27 than Apple’s entire leading-edge portfolio across all chip families. Broadcom, Amazon, and AMD add to this tally.

The structural implication is clear. Apple built TSMC’s leading-edge business over the past decade, funding yield learning and absorbing startup capacity on every new node. AI customers are now using that infrastructure at scale. Apple remains TSMC’s largest smartphone customer and a critical anchor tenant, but it is no longer the defining force at the bleeding edge. TSMC’s largest growth driver is now Nvidia.

### TSMC’s Platform Shift: HPC Overtakes Mobile

For most of TSMC’s history, smartphones were the anchor platform. From 2012 to 2021, smartphone revenue exceeded HPC revenue every single year, driven by Apple and Qualcomm scaling their SoCs on successive leading-edge nodes. For over a decade, Apple was the undisputed anchor tenant at TSMC’s leading edge. From 2012 to 2021, smartphones (led by Apple and Qualcomm) generated more foundry revenue than any other platform.

This was dominance by design: Apple funded yield learning, absorbed startup capacity, and extracted the best economics before competitors could reach volume. The model worked because no other customer could match Apple’s combination of volume, predictability, and willingness to pay.

That era is ending.

But 2022 marked a structural inflection. HPC revenue surpassed smartphones for the first time and has accelerated since, growing at nearly 22% CAGR versus 11% for smartphones. By 2025, HPC revenue is projected to exceed $71 billion, more than double smartphone revenue of $35 billion. The gap is widening. AI accelerators from NVIDIA, AMD, and hyperscaler custom silicon have fundamentally reordered TSMC’s customer mix. Smartphones remain TSMC’s second-largest platform, but they are no longer the growth engine.

This shift has direct implications for Apple. Despite being the largest smartphone customer at TSMC, Apple’s leverage has diminished as HPC customers absorb disproportionate leading-edge capacity. Apple’s share of total TSMC revenue fell from 24% in 2023 to 17% in 2025, even as Apple’s absolute spend continued rising. The countervailing trend is revenue per smartphone, which has tripled since 2012 and reached nearly $28 by 2025. Each iPhone now generates 3x the foundry revenue it did a decade ago, reflecting larger dies, more advanced nodes, and increasing silicon content (M-series Macs, AirPods, Apple Watch). Apple may be losing share of TSMC’s total output, but it is extracting more value per device than ever before.

Apple is not standing still. The 1.4nm ramp in 2029 shows Apple reclaiming 67% node share, suggesting strategic capacity reservations made years in advance. Rumors of Baltra, Apple’s server-class silicon for internal AI infrastructure, point to a potential expansion into HPC territory. And revenue per smartphone has tripled since 2012 as die sizes grow and silicon content expands across the product portfolio. Apple may lose relative share of TSMC’s output, but it is extracting more value per device than ever before. The question is whether that is enough. When AI wafer demand is growing at 2x Apple’s rate and hyperscalers are writing multi-billion dollar prepayment checks, even the world’s most important smartphone customer finds itself competing for attention at the foundry. The AI wave is too big to ignore, and Apple’s position at TSMC, while still formidable, is no longer uncontested.

Apple alone took >50% of 20nm, 16nm, N5, and of N3 at each node’s respective peak.
